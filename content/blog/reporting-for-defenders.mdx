Good reporting is operational work, not administrative cleanup. The quality of the report shapes how fast the receiving team can validate, prioritize, and fix what you found.

## Start with the attack path

A list of isolated findings forces the reader to do the attacker thinking for you. A better structure is to explain how the issues connect, what assumptions enable the chain, and what the likely impact looks like if an attacker is patient.

## Separate proof from noise

Evidence matters, but too much of it in the wrong place slows readers down. Keep the main narrative tight. Put supporting screenshots, requests, and reproduction notes where technical teams can find them without burying the executive view.

## Write remediation like you expect it to be implemented

The worst remediation guidance is vague but confident. Good guidance tells teams what to change, where the control should live, and what follow-up validation should prove after the fix is deployed.

Reporting is part of the engagement itself. If the report is weak, the engagement is incomplete.
