Automation gets sold as a cure for analyst fatigue, but the real value is narrower than that. It is excellent for repeated collection, normalization, and change detection. It is much less reliable when it pretends to replace investigation.

## Automate the boring edges

Anything repetitive, deterministic, and easy to verify is a good automation target. Asset collection, formatting, deduplication, and evidence packaging all fit that pattern.

## Keep the decision points human

Risk narratives, exploitability judgments, and business-context interpretation are not just data processing tasks. They depend on tradeoffs and system understanding. If you automate that layer too aggressively, you create fast but shallow output.

## Design around analyst control

The best security workflows still make it easy for a human to override, annotate, and redirect the system. That is the difference between useful tooling and a noisy black box.

Automation should increase clarity and speed. It should not flatten the work into checkbox output.
